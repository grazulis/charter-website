Liverpool City Region

A Community Charter on Data & AI

Eleven principles, written by 59 residents, on how data and AI should work for the people of the Liverpool City Region.

Read the charter

Charter overview

Eleven principles.
One simple ask: data and AI that work for people.

The Charter outlines how data and AI can work for Liverpool City Region residents — and how it shouldn’t. It is a request for human integrity and dignity to be the core value behind every project.

The principles do not supersede legal responsibilities. They describe how to put trustworthiness into practice. Residents want the Liverpool City Region to lead the way in community-focussed data and AI.

Some principles may already be standard practice. In those cases the Charter outlines where residents want to know more about what is already happening. Data and AI projects fail when they are unclear, untrustworthy and short-sighted. Following these principles helps prevent that — and supports radical transparency on how the technologies are used.

“My hope is that, if used with the best interests of the public in mind, data and AI can drastically improve healthcare, public services, education and local services.”

The Principles

How residents want data and AI to be used.

Each principle below is followed by what it means in practice. Together they describe a community-led standard for trustworthy data and AI.

  1. 01

    Beneficial

    Use data and AI for the good and benefit of the community and the Liverpool City Region. Where possible, benefits should reach wider society as well.

    Read moreRead less

    People and organisations should prioritise public benefit in all data and AI projects. Public good can mean a lot of things, from operational efficiencies to saving lives. Residents especially want to see data and AI used to improve existing services. While projects should focus on the City Region first, residents would like to see other areas benefit from the research and projects developed here.

  2. 02

    Security

    Ensure that the Five Safes — Safe Data, Projects, People, Settings, and Outputs — and UK GDPR are being adhered to.

    Read moreRead less

    It is expected that personal data about residents is kept safe. Projects and organisations should keep personal data secure when stored, transmitted and processed. The Five Safes should be core principles for personal and identifiable data safety and security. All organisations must already follow UK General Data Protection Regulations for information security.

  3. 03

    Accountability

    Ensure accountability at all levels, including a declaration of responsibility for each data and AI project.

    Read moreRead less

    Design in accountability from the start. It should be clear who takes responsibility when something goes wrong in data and AI development and decision-making. That means defining who is responsible for: addressing bias in datasets and algorithms; correcting and updating algorithms; monitoring algorithms that are in use; and training front-line staff on algorithm use, benefits and limits. Make it clear to service users who should be contacted if someone wants to challenge a decision made by an algorithm.

  4. 04

    Transparency

    Inspire trust between organisations and residents by being honest in how data is collected, used, and implemented in projects.

    Read moreRead less

    Residents want to be able to know what is happening with their data at scale. Transparency is a key building block of trustworthy practice. It is expected that data and AI projects, and how they use data, are reported in a publicly accessible register. Descriptions of projects must be written in plain English and understandable to residents.

  5. 05

    Inclusivity

    Promote fairness, universal access, and equity in the development of data and AI innovation. Ensure diverse and affected communities are involved and heard throughout the life of the project.

    Read moreRead less

    Residents want everyone to benefit from data and AI projects. State clearly who data and AI projects are intended to benefit. When relevant, consult and engage that community throughout the project. Ensure data and AI projects do not reinforce existing inequities.

  6. 06

    Privacy

    Protect the dignity, identities and privacy of LCR residents.

    Read moreRead less

    Privacy, in this case, is about residents’ ability to live a life free of the negative impacts of data and AI. There should always be an option to opt out of data sharing and AI use. This also includes keeping sensitive information private. Make sure that data re-identification does not harm communities. Data and AI usage in the LCR should not make community hardships and inequities worse.

  7. 07

    Legality

    Keep up to date on legislation and policy changes on data and AI. Always abide by and adhere to the rule of law.

    Read moreRead less

    Data and AI law are constantly developing in the UK. Under existing law, data breaches and misuse must be reported within organisations. They must then be escalated to the Information Commissioner’s Office where appropriate. All organisations are expected to meet the existing legal requirements for secure and safe data sharing. Examples include UK GDPR, the Common Law Duty of Confidentiality for health, and broader legislation like the Equality Act 2010.

  8. 08

    Trustworthy

    Communicate outcomes through a public register of data and AI projects. Flag projects which use AI to raise awareness.

    Read moreRead less

    It is expected that organisations and projects report back on the outcomes of their data and AI use in a publicly accessible register. This is a key way to demonstrate trustworthy practice as it shows alignment to the other principles. For residents to trust organisational use of data and AI it must be understandable. That means flagging deployed projects which use AI.

  9. 09

    Governance

    Ensure that actions have consequences. Innovation with data and AI carries risks; harms and risks must be appropriately managed throughout the lifecycle of a project.

    Read moreRead less

    Project descriptions should include sharing information on risks and mitigations. Projects could lose the stamp of the Charter if they do not report on the risks and mitigations of their data use. The three original signing bodies are responsible for publicly reporting on these data and AI projects, including risks, in the aforementioned public register. They should create processes for overseeing this requirement.

  10. 10

    Balanced Innovation

    Use data and AI to drive innovation in the LCR — but counteract negative impacts on the environment and the wellbeing of future generations.

    Read moreRead less

    Residents want the LCR to lead the way in community-led data innovation. Data silos and bureaucratic process should not prevent this progress. However, innovation should not cause harm. While AI may mean job losses, every effort should be made to retrain and upskill the local workforce. Residents want to see their community and environment protected. Environmental sustainability should be addressed in digital and physical data infrastructure.

  11. 11

    Oversight

    An independent review takes place at minimum every two years on partners’ use of the Charter. Changes to the Charter are informed by this review.

    Read moreRead less

    Data and AI technologies are constantly evolving. It is expected that the Charter evolves with them as a living document. A review of the Charter and signing organisations’ use of data and AI is expected at least every two years. Organisations and projects will be kitemarked to the 11 principles. This review must be independent of signing organisations and inclusive of residents to ensure external oversight.

Created in Liverpool

59 residents. Four days. One Charter.

The Assembly was set up by the LCR Civic Data Cooperative — a project funded by the Liverpool City Region Combined Authority and hosted by the Civic Health Innovation Labs at the University of Liverpool.

Over four days, residents from across the City Region came together to ask what trustworthy data and AI should look like in their community — and to write the principles that became this Charter.

Read more in the Full Report and Charter by Dr Emily Rempel, or the Independent Evaluation Report by Anna Beckett.

The future

A living Charter.

Creating the Charter is only part of the job in making AI work for the region. A biannual review will be completed, with the first happening mid-2027. That means understanding whether the Charter is an effective social licence on data and AI use.
